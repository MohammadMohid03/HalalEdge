📖 What is Halal Investing?
Halal investing refers to making investment decisions that comply with Islamic Shariah law. It avoids interest (riba), excessive uncertainty (gharar), and businesses involved in prohibited activities.
🚫
What Makes a Stock Haram?
Learn which industries and financial characteristics disqualify a stock from Shariah compliance — alcohol, gambling, conventional banking and more.
Read more →
A stock is considered haram if the company's primary business involves: (1) Alcohol production or distribution, (2) Conventional banking or insurance that charges/pays riba, (3) Gambling or casinos, (4) Pork products, (5) Weapons manufacturing, (6) Pornography or adult entertainment, (7) Tobacco. Even otherwise permissible companies can be screened out if their debt ratio exceeds 33% or haram revenue exceeds 5% of total revenue.
✅
What Makes a Stock Halal?
Understand the positive criteria: permissible business activity, low debt, minimal haram revenue, and passing the AAOIFI financial ratio tests.
Read more →
For a stock to be considered halal: (1) The core business must be permissible (tech, healthcare, real estate, consumer goods, etc.), (2) Total interest-bearing debt must be less than 33% of total assets, (3) Revenue from haram activities must be less than 5%, (4) Accounts receivable and cash must not exceed 49% of total assets. Companies like Apple, Microsoft, and Google typically pass these screens comfortably.
💸
What is Riba (Interest)?
Riba is the Islamic prohibition on interest. Understand why it matters for investing and how to avoid companies with excessive interest-based financing.
Read more →
Riba literally means "increase" or "excess" in Arabic and refers to any guaranteed interest payment on money lent or borrowed. In investing, this means: avoiding bonds (which pay fixed interest), avoiding conventional banks (whose business model is riba), and scrutinizing companies that carry excessive interest-bearing debt. Companies that earn significant interest income on cash holdings can also become problematic. HalalEdge automatically flags all riba-related issues.
🌍

The global Islamic finance industry surpassed $4 trillion in assets in 2024, driven by growth in GCC countries, Malaysia, Indonesia, and the United Kingdom. ☽ AAOIFI Shariah Standards
HalalEdge uses AAOIFI (Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions) standards — the global benchmark for Islamic finance compliance.
Financial Ratio Tests
• Debt ratio ≤ 33% of total assets
• Receivables ≤ 49% of total assets
• Haram revenue ≤ 5% of total revenue
• Interest income ≤ 5% of total revenue
Prohibited Sectors
• Alcohol & tobacco production
• Conventional banking & insurance
• Gambling & casinos
• Pork & non-halal food
• Adult entertainment & weapons

📈 Investing Basics for Beginners
New to investing? Start here. These articles cover everything from what a stock is to how to read financial ratios.
🏢
What is a Stock?
A stock represents ownership in a company. When you buy shares, you become a part-owner and share in its profits and growth.
Read more →
When a company wants to raise money, it can sell ownership stakes to the public through shares. If Apple has 15 billion shares outstanding and you own 10 shares, you own 10/15,000,000,000 of Apple Inc. As the company grows and earns more profit, each share becomes more valuable. This is why halal investing in stocks is permissible — you are a real owner sharing in real business profits, not charging or paying interest.
📊
Understanding P/E Ratio
The Price-to-Earnings ratio tells you how much you're paying for each dollar of a company's earnings. Learn to use it wisely.
Read more →
P/E = Stock Price ÷ Earnings Per Share. If a stock trades at $100 and earns $5 per share, the P/E is 20x — meaning you pay $20 for every $1 of earnings. A high P/E (40x+) means investors expect fast growth. A low P/E (10x–15x) suggests slower growth or undervaluation. Compare P/E to industry peers, not across sectors — tech companies naturally trade at higher P/Es than utilities.
⚖️
Diversification in Islam
Is diversification permissible in Islam? Yes — and it's actually recommended. Learn how to build a diversified halal portfolio.
Read more →
Diversification — spreading investments across multiple halal companies and sectors — is fully permissible and encouraged in Islamic finance. The Prophet (ﷺ) said "Do not put all your eggs in one basket" (paraphrased from various hadith about risk management). A well-diversified halal portfolio typically includes technology, healthcare, consumer goods, industrials, and real estate sectors — all avoiding haram industries.
🎯
Long-term vs Short-term
Is day trading halal? What about long-term investing? Understand the Islamic perspective on different investment horizons.
Read more →
Long-term investing (months to years) is generally considered more appropriate in Islamic finance as it reflects genuine ownership and participation in business growth. Day trading is more controversial — some scholars permit it if no riba is involved (e.g. no margin trading), while others discourage it due to excessive speculation (gharar). HalalEdge's AI predictions are calibrated for medium-term (1–12 month) investment horizons.
